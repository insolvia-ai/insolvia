Regulatory Source Register

The non-negotiable half of the feature list. For each feature, this maps the single authoritative source of truth — the statute, court rule, official form, dataset, or third-party interface that dictates correct behavior — so you know, before writing code, whether you're facing a data problem, an integration problem, or a form-replication problem.

Scope U.S. consumer & business bankruptcy Compiled 2026-07-15 Verify before build — sources change on fixed cycles

How to read this

Every feature is tagged by the kind of engineering problem the regulation creates. This is the most useful first cut: it tells you which team owns it, where the risk sits, and what "done" means. Most of the hard features carry more than one tag.

FORM

Replicate an official Judicial Conference form field-for-field. "Done" = matches the published PDF exactly. Breaks whenever the form is revised.

DATA

Maintain a government dataset that changes on a fixed calendar. Correctness is legal, not cosmetic. Needs a refresh pipeline, not just a one-time load.

LOGIC

Implement a calculation or rule defined in statute. "Done" = produces the legally correct number. Needs test cases drawn from real filings.

INTEGRATION

Build to a third party's API/contract you don't control (courts, bureaus, course providers). Their spec is your requirement; get their docs first.

WORKFLOW

Mostly user-defined behavior with light regulatory constraint. Belongs to Track B (user/workflow requirements), listed here only for completeness.

⏱ The maintenance calendar — the biggest ongoing obligation

Several datasets are only valid on the day you pull them. A filing computed with stale figures can be rejected or expose a client. Build these as scheduled refreshes with an effective-date field, and treat each update as a release.

Case Preparation

The regulatory core of the product. Almost everything here is dictated by official forms, statutory calculations, and government datasets — very little is yours to invent.

Form Editor & PreviewB101, B106A–J, B107…
FORM
 Official Forms approved by the Judicial Conference; issued under FRBP 9009. Mandatory, nationwide. uscourts.gov — Bankruptcy Forms Revised ~Dec 1 cycle + interim Mediumcorrectness = exact match
In plain termsA bankruptcy case is filed on a fixed set of government forms: the petition (the request to open the case), the schedules (itemized lists of everything the debtor owns and everyone they owe), and statements (written financial disclosures, like recent income and money transfers). This feature lets staff fill those in and check them before filing. Because the forms are standardized for every court in the country, your job is to reproduce them exactly — an off-spec form gets bounced by the court.
Common Creditor List
WORKFLOW
 Internal reusable data. No governing authority — but feeds the creditor matrix, so address format must satisfy court noticing. Internal · matrix format per local court rules n/a Low
In plain termsA creditor is anyone the debtor owes money to. The same banks, credit-card companies, and collection agencies show up case after case, so a shared list saves re-typing them every time. It's mostly a convenience feature. The one outside constraint: these names and addresses feed the creditor matrix (the mailing list the court uses to notify everyone), so addresses have to be formatted the way the court expects.
Exemption Analyzer
DATALOGIC
 11 U.S.C. §522 (federal set) plus all 50 states' schemes & opt-out rules. Amounts adjust under §104(b). 11 U.S.C. §522 · Fed. Register §104 notice Fed: Apr 1 / 3 yrs · State: ad hoc Highwrong = client loses assets
In plain termsExemptions are the legal protections that let a debtor keep certain property — a home, a car, work tools — instead of it being sold to pay creditors. Every state has its own list of what's protected and up to what dollar value, and there's a federal list too; often the debtor must choose one set or the other. This is high-stakes: choosing wrong can cost a client their house. On top of that, the dollar limits rise for inflation every three years, so the figures must be kept current.
Means Test CalculatorB122A-1, B122A-2
FORMDATALOGIC
 11 U.S.C. §707(b). Uses IRS Standards + Census median income. Legally required eligibility test. justice.gov/ust — Means Testing Data 2–4×/yr · Form Dec 1 Highwrong = rejected filing
In plain termsThe means test is a required calculation that decides whether someone earns too much to use the simpler Chapter 7 (debts wiped out in a few months) and must instead use Chapter 13 (a multi-year repayment plan). It compares the debtor's income against their state's median income, then subtracts standardized living-expense allowances published by the IRS. It's legally required and easy to get wrong — a miscalculation can disqualify the filing or draw a challenge from the trustee.
Paycheck Calculator
LOGIC
 Feeds Current Monthly Income (§101(10A)). Net-pay math from federal/state withholding & payroll deductions. IRS Pub. 15 (withholding) · state tax tables Annual (tax year) Medium
In plain termsThis works out a debtor's real take-home pay from their pay stub — gross wages minus taxes and payroll deductions — and feeds that figure into the means test and the income schedules. Income is the number everything else keys off: it decides both whether someone qualifies for Chapter 7 and how much a Chapter 13 debtor has to repay, so it needs to be right.
Informational Charts
DATA
 Reference views (exemption caps, median-income tables). Derived from the same authoritative datasets — must cite an effective date. Derived from §522 & UST data feeds Follows source data Low
In plain termsBuilt-in reference tables — things like exemption limits and income thresholds — so staff can look up a number without leaving the app or hunting through a government website. They're only as trustworthy as the day they were last updated, so each chart should clearly show the date it took effect.
IRS & Census Data
DATA
 The pipeline itself. IRS National/Local Standards (via BLS CES) + Census median family income, republished by the UST as XLS/XLSX. justice.gov/ust — Means Testing (download files) Effective-dated releases Higheverything downstream
In plain termsThese are the behind-the-scenes datasets the means test relies on: IRS "standards" for how much a household is allowed for living expenses (food, housing, transportation), and Census figures for median income by state and household size. The government reissues them on a set schedule, and this feature is the pipeline that keeps your copy up to date. If it goes stale, every means test computed from it is wrong — which is why it's marked high-risk.
Chapter 7 Cases
FORMLOGIC
 11 U.S.C. ch. 7 — liquidation. Core consumer case; drives means test + Schedule C exemptions. Title 11, U.S. Code Statutory (rare) Medium
In plain termsThe most common consumer bankruptcy, called "liquidation." Eligible debts are erased within a few months in exchange for giving up any property that isn't protected by an exemption. This is core functionality — it ties directly to the means test (who qualifies) and the exemptions (what they keep).
Chapter 11 Business Cases
FORMLOGIC
 11 U.S.C. ch. 11 — reorganization. Non-individual form set (B201 etc.); Subchapter V variant for small business. Non-individual forms (B2xx) Statutory (rare) Medium
In plain termsBusiness reorganization — a company keeps operating while it restructures its debts, rather than shutting down. It uses a different set of forms than consumer cases (the "non-individual" forms). There's also a streamlined version called Subchapter V aimed at small businesses. Only needed by firms that handle business clients, not just individuals.
Chapter 13 Plan CalculatorB122C-1/2, B113
FORMLOGIC
 11 U.S.C. ch. 13. National plan form B113 or a district's local plan; disposable-income math must reconcile. Form B113 + local plan forms Form Dec 1 · local forms vary Highwrong = plan rejected
In plain termsChapter 13 is for individuals who repay their debts over three to five years instead of liquidating. The plan lays out the monthly payment, and the math has to reconcile income, expenses, and debts across the whole plan period — if it doesn't add up, the court rejects the plan and it has to be redone. Watch out: many court districts require their own local plan form rather than the national one.
Chapters 9 & 12
FORMLOGIC
 ch. 9 municipal · ch. 12 family farmer/fisherman. Niche; ch. 12 has its own plan & discharge (B3180F). Title 11, U.S. Code Statutory (rare) Lowdefer unless targeted
In plain termsTwo specialized case types: Chapter 9 is for municipalities (cities, towns, school districts) that can't pay their debts, and Chapter 12 is a repayment plan built specifically for family farmers and fishermen. Both are low-volume and only matter if you serve those clients — safe to postpone unless you're deliberately targeting them.
Case Templates
WORKFLOW
 Firm-defined presets over the official form sets. No external authority beyond the forms they pre-fill. Internal n/a Low
In plain termsPre-filled starting points for common situations (say, "single filer, no property, Chapter 7"), so staff don't build every new case from a blank slate. Purely an internal time-saver layered on top of the official forms — no outside rules apply.
Chapter 15marked "upcoming"
FORMLOGIC

Electronic Filing

Pure integration work, and the one place where an external party's spec can constrain your whole data model. Get the CM/ECF developer docs and IEPD packages before finalizing how you store case data — you must emit their XML shape.

FeatureTypeGoverning authorityCanonical sourceUpdate cadenceRisk
OneTouch Initial Filing
INTEGRATION
 NextGen CM/ECF. Submit petition as XML (IEPD) + PDF (PDF is the official record). Auth via PACER Authentication API. Per-court variation. PACER Developer Resources (IEPD 1.7) Per IEPD release (~yearly) Highexternal dependency
In plain termsBankruptcy cases are filed electronically through the federal courts' system, called CM/ECF (accessed via PACER, the courts' login/records portal). This feature submits the opening petition in essentially one step. The catch is that you're building to the court's rules, not your own: they expect the case data in their specific XML format plus a PDF (the PDF is the official record), login runs through PACER, and each court can differ slightly. That external dependency is why it's high-risk.
Post-Petition Filing
INTEGRATION
 Same CM/ECF web services for amendments, motions, schedules after case opening. Event/docket codes differ by court. PACER Developer Resources Per IEPD release Medium
In plain termsA case keeps generating documents after it opens — corrections (amendments), requests to the court (motions), and extra schedules. This files those through the same court system, so the entire case stays electronic instead of just the opening petition. Each court labels these filings with its own event codes, which is the main wrinkle.

Client & Case Management

A mix: deadline/claims/noticing features carry real procedural rules; the rest is workflow. Note the compliance overlay on billing and the client portal even where the core feature is user-defined.

Court Notices & Calendar
INTEGRATIONLOGIC
 Ingest court notices (NEF). Deadline math governed by FRBP 9006 time computation + rule-driven bar dates. CM/ECF NEF · Fed. Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure Rules ~Dec 1 Highmissed deadline = malpractice
In plain termsThroughout a case the court sends out notices and sets deadlines (for hearings, objections, filing documents). Missing one can seriously hurt a client — it can even be malpractice — which is why this is high-risk. The feature pulls in those court notices automatically and calculates the resulting deadlines. It's not a plain calendar: the rules for counting the days are set by formal court procedure, so the date math has to follow them precisely.
Claim TrackerB410, B410A
FORMINTEGRATION
 Proof of Claim (Official Form 410) & the court's claims register. Reconcile filed claims against schedules. Form B410 · CM/ECF claims register Form Dec 1 Medium
In plain termsOnce a case opens, creditors file a "proof of claim" — a formal statement of exactly what they say they're owed. This feature tracks those claims and lets the attorney or trustee compare them against what the debtor listed on their schedules, so mismatches (a creditor claiming more than the debtor recorded) get caught. The proof-of-claim document is itself a standardized official form.

Case Intake (Questionnaire)
WORKFLOW
 Firm-defined intake that feeds official forms. Field coverage should map to schedule/SOFA requirements. Internal · mapped to B106/B107 n/a Low
In plain termsA structured form the client fills out at the start, so you collect their full financial picture once instead of chasing details later. You design the questions — but design them so they map onto everything the official schedules and statements will eventually need. Do that well and the forms almost fill themselves; miss fields and staff spend the case circling back for more info.
Legal Noticing Services
INTEGRATION
 Required creditor notice under FRBP 2002. The courts' Bankruptcy Noticing Center (BNC) is the standard channel. FRBP 2002 · BNC (ebn.uscourts.gov) Rules ~Dec 1 Mediumdue-process notice
In plain termsThe law requires that creditors and other affected parties be formally notified of the case and its key events — it's a due-process requirement, so everyone owed money gets a fair chance to respond. This feature handles that mailing. A court rule spells out what notice is required, and the courts run a standard mailing service (the Bankruptcy Noticing Center) that most filers plug into rather than mailing everything themselves.
Invoices & Paymentsmarked "upcoming"
WORKFLOWLOGIC
 Attorney-fee disclosure is regulated: §329 & FRBP 2016(b) require disclosing compensation. Billing UI is otherwise yours. 11 U.S.C. §329 · FRBP 2016 Statutory (rare) Mediumfee disclosure
In plain termsBuilt-in billing so a firm can collect its fees without separate software. Most of the billing screen is yours to design — but there's a bankruptcy-specific wrinkle: attorneys are legally required to disclose to the court what they were paid for the case. So the billing side needs to support producing that disclosure, which is why it carries a compliance note.

Credit Report & Course Integration

Both are third-party integrations, but of different kinds: one is a commercial data feed with heavy consumer-protection compliance, the other is a government-maintained approved-provider requirement.

FeatureTypeGoverning authorityCanonical sourceUpdate cadenceRisk
Credit Report Integration
INTEGRATIONLOGIC
 Tri-merge (Experian/Equifax/TransUnion), usually via a reseller. Governed by FCRA permissible-purpose & GLBA. Parse into Schedules D/E/F + matrix. Bureau/reseller API contract · FCRA (15 U.S.C. §1681) Per vendor contract HighFCRA compliance + vendor
In plain termsPulls the debtor's credit report — ideally a "tri-merge" that combines all three big credit bureaus (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion) — to auto-fill the list of debts and creditors instead of typing each one by hand. Two things make it high-risk: credit data is tightly regulated (you need a legitimate legal purpose to pull it and must meet consumer-protection and privacy rules), and you access it through a contract with a bureau or reseller, so you're depending on an outside vendor's system and terms.
Bankruptcy Course Integration
INTEGRATION
 Pre-filing credit counseling & pre-discharge debtor education. Providers must be §111-approved by the UST (BA in AL & NC). Certificate is a filing requirement. justice.gov/ust — Approved providers List changes continuously Mediuminvalid cert blocks discharge
In plain termsThe law makes debtors take two short courses: credit counseling before they file, and a financial-management course before their debts are wiped out. Each course issues a completion certificate that must be filed, and only government-approved providers count. This feature connects to those providers so a certificate never gets missed — and it matters that the approved-provider list changes constantly, because a certificate from a since-removed provider won't be accepted and can block the debtor's discharge.

What this changes about how you plan

The register replaces guesswork with reading

For every FORM / DATA / LOGIC row, there is no "gathering requirements from users" step — the requirement is published. The work is disciplined desk research: open the canonical source, extract the fields/formula/dataset, and record its effective date. Assign an owner to build out each row into the exact field list or algorithm.

Your real product risk is staleness, not features

The high-risk rows (means test, exemptions, deadlines, e-filing) are dangerous mainly because they go out of date on a schedule you don't control. Design an effective-date model and a scheduled-refresh process from day one — retrofitting it later is painful, and a filing computed on stale data is the failure mode that hurts clients.

State exemptions & local court variation are the hidden iceberg

Two rows quietly hide enormous scope: 50-state exemption schemes and per-district local rules/forms (e.g. local Chapter 13 plans, event codes). Neither has a single feed. Decide early which states/districts you support at launch and stage the rest — this is where a subject-matter expert earns their fee.

Get the CM/ECF and bureau specs before you model data

The two INTEGRATION-heavy areas — court e-filing (IEPD XML) and credit bureaus — impose data shapes on you. Request their developer documentation and, for the bureaus, the reseller/FCRA compliance requirements before finalizing your case schema, so you're not re-shaping storage to fit their payloads later.

Sources & methodology

Two different kinds of link appear in this document. The "Canonical source" column in each table points to where you'd pull the live, authoritative data or spec at build time. This section is different: it's the research trail — the pages actually consulted to decide how each feature was classified, what it's governed by, and how risky it is. Provenance and honesty about confidence matter here, so each source is marked Primary · verified (official government source read this session), Secondary · context (industry/commercial source used for how-it-works context, not legal fact), or, further down, Verify for claims drawn from general knowledge that were not independently confirmed this session.

Where the feature list itself came from

Important for provenance: the ~30 features were not chosen by me. They come from your business partner's analysis of a bankruptcy-software vendor's desktop-vs-cloud comparison grid (checkmarks per feature per platform; several items flagged "Upcoming Feature," apparently tied to the cloud edition). My work was to classify those given features, identify what governs each, and rate the risk — not to invent the list. The raw grid's per-platform checkmark availability was not fully preserved in that analysis, so desktop-vs-cloud availability per feature is still an open item to confirm from the vendor page.

What is analysis, not citation

Finally, several parts of this document are my judgment, synthesized from the above rather than quoted from any one source — you should weigh them as opinion: the FORM / DATA / LOGIC / INTEGRATION / WORKFLOW classification scheme; the High / Medium / Low risk ratings; the data-vs-integration-vs-form framing; and the planning recommendations (MVP sequencing, the "staleness is your real risk" argument, staging states/districts). These are reasoning aids, not established facts.

Sources are official/primary where possible — uscourts.gov (forms & rules), justice.gov/ust (means testing, approved providers), pacer.uscourts.gov (e-filing developer resources), Title 11 U.S. Code, and the Federal Register (§104 adjustments).
